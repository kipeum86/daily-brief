Editor's View

A narrative layer for the numbers and headlines

This Week’s Core Theme

Geopolitical de-escalation and a semiconductor resurgence provided a powerful catalyst for global equities this week. A ceasefire agreement between the U.S. and Iran triggered a sharp recalibration in energy markets, sending oil prices tumbling and volatility plunging. This risk-on environment disproportionately benefited tech-heavy indices and South Korean markets, which were further buoyed by aggressive domestic fiscal stimulus.

Market Review

The easing of Middle Eastern tensions caused WTI crude to plunge 13.42% to $96.57 a barrel, while the VIX volatility index retreated 19.44%. Relieved of immediate energy-driven inflation fears, U.S. equities advanced, with the S&P 500 rising 3.56% and the Nasdaq adding 4.68%. This rally materialized despite Federal Reserve minutes indicating a growing openness to further rate hikes.

South Korea’s KOSPI was the global standout, surging 8.96% to 5,858.87. The index was propelled by a semiconductor supercycle—highlighted by Samsung’s massive revenue gains—and the passage of a 26.2 trillion won supplementary budget. The Bank of Korea maintained its base rate at 2.50% for a seventh consecutive meeting, supporting domestic liquidity. Consequently, the Korean won strengthened, pushing the USD/KRW pair down 1.76% to 1,482.70. Elsewhere, gold advanced 2.92% to $4,787.40, and Bitcoin rose 3.56%, reflecting broad-based asset demand in a lower-volatility environment.

Top Stories

  • Geopolitics & Energy: A U.S.-Iran ceasefire agreement dramatically reduced geopolitical risk premiums, prompting a steep sell-off in global crude markets and lifting equities.
  • Korean Stimulus & Tech: The National Assembly passed a 26.2 trillion won supplementary budget to support lower-income households. Concurrently, Samsung’s semiconductor revenues reportedly surpassed Google’s, driving local market euphoria.
  • Monetary Policy: The BOK froze rates at 2.50%, while U.S. Fed minutes revealed policymakers remain open to further tightening to manage underlying economic pressures.
  • Domestic Credit Risks: Despite the equity boom, South Korean bank credit card loan delinquency rates reached a 21-year high, signaling underlying consumer stress.

What to Watch Next Week

Markets will monitor whether the U.S.-Iran ceasefire holds and establishes a new, lower floor for energy prices. In Asia, investors will assess the inflationary impact of Seoul’s massive supplementary budget against the backdrop of rising consumer delinquencies. Globally, renewed tariff threats from Donald Trump targeting European automakers may reintroduce trade friction into an otherwise buoyant market.
