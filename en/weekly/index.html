Editor's View

A narrative layer for the numbers and headlines

This Week’s Core Theme

Geopolitical relief acted as a powerful catalyst for global markets this week, triggering a sharp divergence between plunging energy prices and surging equities. A 10-day ceasefire agreement between Israel and Lebanon served as a pressure release valve, unwinding the geopolitical premium in oil markets and emboldening investors to aggressively bid up risk assets. This sudden de-escalation overshadowed earlier rhetoric regarding a US blockade of the Strait of Hormuz and gloomy macroeconomic warnings from the IMF, giving growth sectors the runway to rally.

Market Review

The unwinding of geopolitical anxiety sent WTI crude plummeting 14.48% to $82.59. This collapse in energy costs, paired with a decline in the US 10-year Treasury yield (-1.64%), provided a massive tailwind for equities. The tech-heavy Nasdaq surged 6.84%, while the S&P 500 added 4.54%.

South Korean markets mirrored this exuberance. The KOSDAQ led global indices with a 6.99% leap, and the KOSPI advanced 5.68%. Volatility retreated, with the VIX dropping 9.10% to 17.48. In the currency markets, the won strengthened slightly, pushing USD/KRW down 0.52% to 1,465.68. Meanwhile, Bitcoin rallied 6.81% to clear $75,500, and gold maintained its structural appeal, rising 2.47%.

Top Stories

In the Middle East, the Israel-Lebanon ceasefire dominated headlines, easing immediate supply disruption fears. In Europe, political landscapes shifted as Hungary marked the end of the Orbán era, and the EU launched a critical minerals procurement platform to reduce reliance on China.

On the macroeconomic front, the IMF cut its global growth outlook, warning of a drift toward more adverse scenarios. However, it maintained South Korea’s growth forecast at 1.9%, citing robust semiconductor exports. Domestically, Seoul announced plans for “Mega Special Zones” to deregulate AI, robotics, and bio-industries. Yet, underlying financial stress remains visible, as South Korean bank delinquency rates hit a 10-year high of 0.62% amid whispers of potential rate hikes. In corporate news, Netflix posted a massive earnings beat, accompanied by the exit of Reed Hastings from its board.

What to Watch Next Week

Markets will closely monitor the fragility of the Middle East ceasefire and its ongoing impact on crude pricing. Investors will also weigh the divergence between booming tech equities and underlying credit stress, particularly as South Korean domestic loan rates face upward pressure despite a broader freeze.
